# Motion

## What is Motion?

An object is said to be in **motion** if it changes its position with respect to a reference point over time. If the position does not change, the object is at **rest**. Whether something is at rest or in motion depends on the **frame of reference** — a person sitting in a moving bus is at rest relative to the bus, but in motion relative to the ground.

## Distance and Displacement

When an object moves from one point to another, two quantities describe the path it takes:

- **Distance** is the total length of the path travelled. It is a scalar quantity (has only magnitude). Distance is always positive.
- **Displacement** is the shortest straight-line distance between the initial and final positions, along with direction. It is a vector quantity (has magnitude and direction). Displacement can be zero even if distance is not.

For example, if you walk 3 km north and then 4 km east, your distance is 7 km but your displacement is only 5 km (by the Pythagorean theorem).

## Speed and Velocity

**Speed** tells us how fast an object is moving. It is the distance covered per unit time:

<EquationBlock 
  equation="\text{speed} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}" 
  description="Speed formula" 
/>

Speed is a scalar — it has no direction.

**Velocity** tells us how fast AND in which direction an object is moving:

<EquationBlock 
  equation="\text{velocity} = \frac{\text{displacement}}{\text{time}}" 
  description="Velocity formula" 
/>

Velocity is a vector — it has both magnitude and direction.

**Average speed** is the total distance divided by total time:

<EquationBlock 
  equation="v_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}" 
  description="Average speed formula" 
/>

## Uniform and Non-Uniform Motion

- **Uniform motion**: An object covers equal distances in equal intervals of time. The speed is constant.
- **Non-uniform motion**: An object covers unequal distances in equal intervals of time. The speed keeps changing.

In real life, most motion is non-uniform. A car speeding up, slowing down, or turning is in non-uniform motion.

## Acceleration

When velocity changes, we say the object is **accelerating**. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity:

<EquationBlock 
  equation="a = \frac{v - u}{t}" 
  description="Acceleration formula" 
/>

where:
- a is acceleration
- v is final velocity
- u is initial velocity
- t is time taken

If velocity increases, acceleration is **positive**. If velocity decreases, acceleration is **negative** (also called **deceleration** or retardation).

The SI unit of acceleration is metres per second squared (m/s^2).

## Equations of Motion

For uniformly accelerated motion along a straight line, three equations relate displacement (s), initial velocity (u), final velocity (v), acceleration (a), and time (t):

**First equation:**
<EquationBlock 
  equation="v = u + at" 
  description="First equation of motion" 
/>

**Second equation:**
<EquationBlock 
  equation="s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2" 
  description="Second equation of motion" 
/>

**Third equation:**
<EquationBlock 
  equation="v^2 = u^2 + 2as" 
  description="Third equation of motion" 
/>

These are extremely important — they allow us to solve almost any problem involving straight-line motion with constant acceleration.

## Uniform Circular Motion

When an object moves in a circle at constant speed, it is in **uniform circular motion**. Even though the speed is constant, the velocity is NOT constant because the direction keeps changing. This means there is always an acceleration directed towards the center of the circle, called **centripetal acceleration**.

Examples of uniform circular motion:
- The moon orbiting the Earth
- A stone tied to a string and whirled in a circle
- Electrons orbiting the nucleus (classical model)
