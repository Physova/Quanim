# Force and Laws of Motion

Understanding how forces affect the state of motion of objects

## What is Force?

A force is a push or pull acting on an object that can change its state of motion or shape. Forces can make objects move, stop, speed up, slow down, or change direction. In everyday life, we experience forces constantly — when we push a door, kick a ball, or even just sit on a chair.

Forces are measured in Newtons (N), named after Sir Isaac Newton who formulated the fundamental laws governing motion and force.

> "Every body continues in its state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line unless it is compelled to change that state by forces impressed upon it." 
> — Isaac Newton, Principia Mathematica

## Newton's First Law of Motion (Law of Inertia)

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and direction, unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.

This is also called the law of inertia. Inertia is the natural tendency of an object to resist a change in its state of motion or rest. The mass of an object is a measure of its inertia — heavier objects have more inertia and are harder to move or stop.

### Real Life Examples
- When a bus suddenly starts moving, passengers fall backward because their body was at rest and tends to remain at rest (inertia of rest).
- When a bus suddenly stops, passengers fall forward because their body was in motion and tends to remain in motion (inertia of motion).
- A coin placed on a cardboard on a glass falls into the glass when the cardboard is flicked away quickly.

## Newton's Second Law of Motion

The rate of change of momentum of an object is proportional to the applied unbalanced force in the direction of the force.

<EquationBlock
  equation="F = ma"
  description="Newton's second law of motion"
/>

Where F is force in Newtons, m is mass in kg, and a is acceleration in m/s².

This means that a larger force produces a greater acceleration, and a heavier object needs more force to achieve the same acceleration.

### Momentum

Momentum is the product of mass and velocity of a moving object.

<EquationBlock
  equation="p = mv"
  description="Definition of momentum"
/>

Where p is momentum in kg·m/s, m is mass in kg, and v is velocity in m/s.

The rate of change of momentum gives us force:

<EquationBlock
  equation="F = \frac{mv - mu}{t}"
  description="Force as the rate of change of momentum"
/>

Which simplifies to F = ma when mass is constant.

### Numerical Example

A cricket ball of mass 0.15 kg is moving at 40 m/s. A fielder stops it in 0.1 seconds. What force did the fielder apply?

<EquationBlock
  equation="F = \frac{m(v - u)}{t}"
  description="Force formula"
/>

<EquationBlock
  equation="F = \frac{0.15 \times (0 - 40)}{0.1}"
  description="Substitution of values"
/>

<EquationBlock
  equation="F = 0.15 \times (-400)"
  description="Calculating acceleration component"
/>

<EquationBlock
  equation="F = -60\text{ N}"
  description="Final calculated force (negative sign indicates opposing force)"
/>

The negative sign shows the force was applied opposite to the direction of motion.

## Newton's Third Law of Motion

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. These forces act on two different objects.

### Real Life Examples
- When you push against a wall, the wall pushes back against you with equal force.
- A rocket moves forward by pushing exhaust gases backward.
- When you walk, you push the ground backward with your foot, and the ground pushes you forward.
- When a gun fires a bullet, the bullet goes forward and the gun recoils backward.

### Important Point
Action and reaction forces act on DIFFERENT objects, which is why they do not cancel each other out.

## Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

When two forces acting on an object are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction, they are balanced forces. Balanced forces do NOT change the state of motion.

When forces are unbalanced (one is larger), the object accelerates in the direction of the larger force.

<EquationBlock
  equation="F_{net} = F_1 - F_2"
  description="Net force calculation for two opposing forces"
/>

## Conservation of Momentum

The total momentum of a system of objects remains constant if no external force acts on it.

<EquationBlock
  equation="m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2"
  description="Law of conservation of momentum"
/>

This explains why a heavy truck causes more damage than a small car at the same speed, and why a gun recoils when a bullet is fired.

## Friction

Friction is a force that opposes the relative motion between two surfaces in contact.

<EquationBlock
  equation="f = \mu N"
  description="Friction force formula"
/>

Where f is friction force, μ is the coefficient of friction, and N is the normal force.

### Types of Friction
- **Static friction**: Acts on objects at rest. It is the maximum force before an object starts moving.
- **Kinetic (sliding) friction**: Acts on objects already in motion. It is always less than static friction.
- **Rolling friction**: Much less than sliding friction, which is why wheels were invented!
